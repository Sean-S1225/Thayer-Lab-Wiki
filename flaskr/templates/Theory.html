It's easy to take for granted how much we use sight in scientific research. So much of science is running simulations, watching and seeing what happens, then drawing conclusions and running more test. This becomes impossible when your target of research shrinks below a certain size. Namely, proteins are far too small to be observed directly.

Methods of studying proteins indirectly have developed tremendously over the past century, but still prove incapable of answering some of the most important questions in biology, such as how allosteric actually propogate through a protein, or more specific questions, such as, "does the introduction of the drug PK11000 to the Y220C mutation of p53 restore wild type activity?".

This is where Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations come in. They allow us to probe the lowest levels of protein dynamics to understand this hidden world.

An MD simulation consists of a system of atoms, and a physics model governing the interactions between these atoms. The system typically contains the protein of interest, in a "box" of water. MD simulations tracks the positions and velocities of these atoms over the course of a simulation, which are typically on a nanosecond (one billionth of a second) or microsecond (one millionth of a second) time scale.

From this data, a trajectory is created, which contains the data generated (positions and velocities) in the simulation. This data can then be studied to answer questions such as the ones posed above: the electrostatic energetics of system can be studied to try to observe allosteric pathways; the deviations of the proteins over time can be analyzed to see if the mutated Y220C p53 occupies different conformational states than the wild type or mutated and drugged protein.

The first MD simulation was performed in the late 1950s on simple gasses, and the first MD simulation performed on a protein was done in the 1970s. Due to an explosion in the number of protein structures available and the amount of computational resources available, the number of MD simulations appearing in publications has drastically increased. The number of papers using MD simulations doubled between 2007 and 2017, going from only 400 to over 1000.

There are three main MD simulation packages, Amber, GROMACS, and LAMMPS, though they do essentially the same thing, though LAMMPS is typically for larger systems.

Running an MD simulation typically consists of five steps:

  • tLEaP:
    • Converting a PDB file into a format Amber understands, with the correct parameters
  • Energy Minimization:
    • Gradually loosening the constraints on the system
  • Heating:
    • "Heating" the simulation, i.e., giving velocities to atoms
  • Equilibration:
    • Additional loosening of the simulation
  • Neutral Dynamics:
    • The simulation itself; this is where the data we care about is generated
